Beginning With Hard Things

Today I am beginning something new. For most of my life I pursued what came easily. If something aligned with my natural ability, I leaned into it and avoided what resisted me. The result was not growth but repetition. My life accumulated activity without direction.

This essay begins with a simple organizing principle: Hard things build a life. Easy things decorate it. I am choosing the former.

// Purpose vs Passion

One of the most important distinctions I have encountered is the difference between passion and purpose.

Passion often follows ability. When we are naturally good at something it feels effortless, and that effortlessness can be mistaken for meaning. But passion alone does not organize a life. It only organizes attention.

Purpose works differently.

Purpose is often attached to difficulty. It requires sustained effort and frequently resists us at the beginning. Where passion makes things feel light, purpose adds weight.

The mistake I made for years was confusing the two. I followed what felt good and called it direction. In reality it was avoidance.

// The Value of Hard Things

Hard things change the structure of time.

They stretch a day. They force attention into the present moment. They remove the illusion that progress happens automatically.

Easy pursuits do the opposite. They produce stimulation without formation. The ego grows while the person remains largely unchanged. The distinction eventually became clear to me:

Ease feeds ego. Difficulty forms character.

Once seen clearly, that pattern becomes impossible to ignore.

// The Anesthetic of Easy Pursuits

Looking back honestly, my fascination with ease functioned like an anesthetic.

Instead of confronting deeper questions about direction or responsibility, I recycled familiar intellectual territory. I revisited the same ideas and projects repeatedly, convincing myself that productivity was occurring.

In reality I was circling.

Ecclesiastes describes this condition succinctly: toil under the sun. Movement without transformation.

// A Deeper Connection

The first disruption to this pattern came through an unexpected pairing: faith and mathematics.

When I became a believer in God—what I understand to be the one true God revealed through Jesus Christ—it was not a quiet conversion. It felt closer to wrestling.

Faith forced me to examine my life honestly. And what I saw looked fractured. Responsibilities were ignored. Relationships were taken for granted. My habits were destructive.

At the time I was deeply entangled in stimulant abuse. Amphetamines such as Adderall and Vyvanse had become constant companions, and their psychological effects accumulated quickly: paranoia, racing thoughts, obsessive investigation.

In that state curiosity remained active but judgment often deteriorated. The result was a life that oscillated between genuine inquiry and unhealthy fixation.

// Technology, Surveillance, and Curiosity

My long-standing interest in technology eventually intersected with that unstable period.

While exploring historical intelligence documents released during the Snowden disclosures, I encountered references to NSA programs designed to map large portions of the internet’s infrastructure. One document describing these capabilities was associated with a program called Treasure Map.

For someone already interested in the mechanics of digital networks, this raised a compelling question:
How much of the internet’s infrastructure can be mapped and analyzed at scale?

Curiosity turned into investigation. At times that investigation was thoughtful. At other times it was conducted while I was dealing with untreated mental health issues and heavy stimulant use.

That combination distorts perception.

With time, medication, and stability I can now see that some conclusions from that period were speculative and require more disciplined evaluation. Still, the core question remains intellectually legitimate.

// The Question of Data Infrastructure

Modern artificial intelligence systems rely on extremely large datasets.

At the same time, governments and corporations have spent decades constructing infrastructure designed to collect and analyze data at scale.

These developments invite a natural question:
To what extent are modern machine-learning systems built upon data ecosystems originally created for large-scale monitoring and analysis?

This essay does not claim certainty. It simply recognizes that the intersection between AI development, large datasets, and data-collection infrastructure deserves careful examination. Future investigation should rely on technical documentation, research papers, and verifiable sources rather than intuition alone.

// A Life of Contradictions

I grew up in Utah, an environment widely perceived as structured and culturally conservative.

Yet my interests developed across very different influences: political history, counterculture movements, computer systems, and philosophy. By eighth grade I had already developed an intense interest in history. One teacher eventually allowed me to demonstrate knowledge through discussion rather than conventional assignments.

That pattern—deep curiosity combined with unconventional behavior—followed me into adulthood.

Sometimes it produced insight. Sometimes it produced chaos.

// Personal Turning Points

Several experiences forced me to confront the consequences of my choices. These moments were not philosophical. They were practical.

They showed me that a life organized around ease eventually collapses under its own contradictions. Hard truths become unavoidable.

// Mental Health and Perspective

I live with Bipolar I disorder.

The condition can generate periods of extreme energy and intensity where ideas move rapidly and connections appear everywhere. During those moments the mind feels powerful but judgment is often compromised. Other periods are slower and heavier.

Note that my last hospitalization was self-admitted and within 3 days of new medication I was normal as I knew myself to be 7 years ago before I had any symptoms of bipolar yet. The most glorious part about this was that the medication could be delivered as an injectable and only needed to be taken 1 time a month. Since being on this medication I can clearly remember my past and clearly think through long term actions and control my impulses with relative ease. It’s given me tremendous empathy and self compassion due to the realization of how hard the struggle must’ve been if I lacked so greatly the clarity that came when on good medication.

Needless to say I don’t have the same lust for stimulants and sex and that alone gives me peace and comfort knowing I’m not walking a thin line between lost and longing for more. The months since October have been a slow descent into normalcy and everyday I thank god for the way back home.

// The Greatest Avoidance

The most important topic that wasn’t talked about and needs an introduction is the cancer diagnosis my mom received twice and the psychotic delusions and lack of sane thought that led me to avoid going home during the bulk of treatment and battling cancer which I know my mom needed me for. This is something that can tear me down to pieces the size of grains of sand like it was nothing.

I know in my heart of hearts what I was avoiding was the reality of life and death being closer than commonly perceived and the struggle to let myself be seen and be the cause of my mom being stressed out over my crazy antics, lack of sleep, and pace of living that caused for concern and caused me to circle back and forth over the same old things for years at a time. That was really hard for me to know I was running from when I faced the challenge of having a mom who needed her son while her son felt he needed to step away because he couldn’t be helpful or useful and had no clue what to do to move forward.

I stopped blaming myself for the lack of presence during the first 2 years following the cancer diagnosis LAST WEEK. I admitted my role in something I could’ve swept under the rug to my therapist, what came up was that I feared disappointing my mom and knowing my role now as the one whose job it is to pick up the slack and cover for the weakness that cancer leaves a human being with in the follow up of a battle with cancer. I found that I had no choice but to buckle up and carry on though not keeping calm while I did so.

I didn’t shrivel up last week when I felt those emotions that are known to destroy me and make me want to do things ever so differently with relentless energy.

My mom is an angel and her cancer fight itself is a miracle and the one thing I must acknowledge about those events is that I probably don’t remember the whole story and I probably wasn’t as absent as I believe myself to be. Not only that but if I was living with my mental illness for that long I owe myself some compassion because my parents didn’t raise me in a way that would ever let me slow down or back down from a fight.

So although I consciously avoided home because something there I couldn’t face with a crowd of faces, even though they were my most beloved faces I know, I don’t gain anything from feeling bad about what I didn’t do. It doesn’t make me a good person for feeling bad and towards the end of my unmedicated and unstable 7 years I used the bad feelings as an excuse to justify acting like a bad person when I was capable of better things and my peoples were deserving of better things.

I'm not sure why this came last in the writing process but it is a big part of the story and it will be followed up with a letter to my mother about my mental health, my substance abuse, my regrets, and my plans to change for the better and commitments I’m going to make regardless of the barriers ahead of me.

She deserves it. She can’t be the same person she was before cancer. I can be more for her if I dig deep, so that’s my new job. To be the bridge between who she was able to be and who she can be now. Any deficiencies and parts in disrepair are now my proving grounds and my job is to carry that load for her never again being a reason for her load getting heavier than it already was.

// Choosing Hard Things

This essay is ultimately not about technology or personal history. It is about orientation.

For years I chased what felt natural. Now I intend to pursue what demands growth. Not because suffering is virtuous, but because formation requires resistance.

Hard things reveal weaknesses. They force discipline. They remove illusions. In that sense they are teachers.

// A New Beginning

So today marks the beginning of a different approach.

A life organized not around ease but around deliberate effort. A life where curiosity remains active but is guided by discipline rather than impulse.

Hard things stretch time. Hard things reveal purpose. Hard things force honesty. And if my past has demonstrated anything clearly, it is this:

"The easy path flatters the self.
The difficult path forms the soul."
